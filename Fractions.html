Step 1

What are Multiples?

Multiples are numbers you get when you multiply a number by 1, 2, 3, 4, 5... Think of it like skip-counting!

💡 Multiples of 3 = 3, 6, 9, 12, 15, 18, 21, 24 ...
3691215182124

3×1=3,  3×2=6,  3×3=9,  3×4=12 ...

💡 Multiples of 4 = 4, 8, 12, 16, 20, 24, 28, 32 ...
48121620242832
💡 Multiples go on forever! Every number has an infinite list of multiples.

Step 2

Finding the LCM

The LCM (Lowest Common Multiple) is the smallest number that appears in both lists of multiples.

Example: LCM of 3 and 4

Multiples of 3:

3691215182124

Multiples of 4:

48121620242832
Common multiples: 12, 24, 36 ...
LCM = 12 (the smallest!)

Example: LCM of 6 and 8

Multiples of 6:

612182430364248

Multiples of 8:

816243240485664
LCM = 24
💡 Shortcut: If one number is a multiple of the other (like 3 and 6), the LCM is just the bigger number!

Step 3

What is LCD?

The LCD (Lowest Common Denominator) is simply the LCM of the denominators of two fractions.

⭐ LCD = LCM of the denominators. That's it!

Example 1

LCD of 13 and 25: Denominators are 3 and 5. LCM of 3 and 5 = 15. So LCD = 15

Example 2

LCD of 34 and 56: Multiples of 4: 4, 8, 12... Multiples of 6: 6, 12... LCD = 12

Example 3 — Shortcut!

LCD of 14 and 38: Is 8 a multiple of 4? Yes! So LCD = 8

Step 4

Converting Fractions to the LCD

Once you know the LCD, convert each fraction. The rule: whatever you multiply the bottom by, multiply the top by the same number!

🔑 The fraction's value doesn't change — you're just cutting pieces into smaller equal slices.

Example: Convert 23 and 14 to LCD = 12

23: 3 → 12 (×4), so top ×4 too: 2×43×4 = 812

14: 4 → 12 (×3), so top ×3 too: 1×34×3 = 312
23 = 812 — same amount, smaller pieces:
23
812
Step 5

Putting It All Together

The full process: 1) Find LCD → 2) Convert → 3) Add or subtract → 4) Simplify

Example: 23 + 14

Step 1: LCD = LCM of 3,4 = 12
Step 2: 23 = 812 and 14 = 312
Step 3: 812 + 312 = 1112
Answer: 1112

Lesson 1

Adding Mixed Numbers

Add the whole numbers, then add the fractions. Find a common denominator first if needed. If the fraction part is improper, convert it.

Example 1 — Same denominator

214 + 134
Wholes: 2+1=3. Fracs: 14+34=44=1
3+1 = 4

Example 2 — Different denominators

313 + 256
LCD = 6. Convert: 13=26
Wholes: 3+2=5. Fracs: 26+56=76=116
5+116 = 616
Lesson 2

Subtracting Mixed Numbers

Subtract whole numbers, then fractions. If the fraction being subtracted is larger, regroup (borrow 1).

💡 Regrouping trick: Borrow 1 from the whole number and add it as the denominator over itself. E.g. 516 = 476

Example — Regrouping needed

513 − 223
Can't do 13−23. Regroup!
513 = 443
443 − 223 = 223
Lesson 3

Fraction × Whole Number

Multiply the numerator by the whole number, keep the denominator the same.

34 × 8
Top: 3×8 = 24. Bottom stays 4.
244 = 6
💡 Think of it as repeated addition: 25×3 = 25+25+25
Lesson 4

Fraction × Fraction

Multiply numerators together and denominators together. Simplify the result.

💡 Cross-cancel before multiplying to make it easier!

Example with cross-cancelling

38 × 49
Cross-cancel: 3 & 9 ÷3 → 1 & 3.  4 & 8 ÷4 → 1 & 2
12 × 13 = 16
Lesson 5

Mixed Number × Whole Number

Two methods: Split (multiply whole part and fraction part separately) or Convert to improper fraction first.

Method 1: Split and multiply

234 × 6
Whole: 2×6 = 12
Fraction: 34×6 = 184 = 412
12 + 412 = 1612
Lesson 6

📝 Word Problems

Look for clue words: "total" / "altogether" = add. "Left" / "how much more" = subtract. "Of" = multiply.

Worked example

Sarah used 214 kg of flour for cookies and 138 kg for a cake. How much flour did she use altogether?

"Altogether" → add. LCD = 8.
228 + 138 = 358
Answer: 358 kg
