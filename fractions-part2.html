Rule #1

🚨 "OF" means MULTIPLY

⭐ Whenever you see the word "of" followed by a fraction or quantity, it means ×.

23 of 12  =  23 × 12  =  8
⚠️ This was Saina's WA1 Q7 mistake! "He used 4⁄5 of the prawns" means multiply — NOT subtract 4/5 directly.
Fraction × Whole

Fraction × Whole Number

📌 Divide the whole by the denominator, then multiply by the numerator.
34 × 8  =  (8 ÷ 4) × 3  =  2 × 3  =  6
Fraction × Fraction

Fraction × Fraction

📌 Multiply tops together, then bottoms together.
23 × 35  =  615  =  25

2/3 × 3/5 = 6/15 = 2/5
⚠️ Critical

Proportion vs Quantity — The Big Trap

✅ "of [something]" = MULTIPLY

"Used 4/5 of the prawns"
→ 4/5 × total prawns

❌ Fixed amount = SUBTRACT directly

"Used 3/4 kg of flour"
→ just subtract that number!

📋 Decision rule: Does the sentence say "of [the thing]"? → multiply.
Is it a number with units (kg, m, $)? → use that value directly.

Concept

Dividing a Fraction by a Whole Number

📌 Method: Multiply the denominator by the whole number.

34 ÷ 6  =  34×6  =  324  =  18
💡 Shortcut: If the numerator is divisible by the whole number, divide numerator directly.

89 ÷ 4  =  29  (since 8 ÷ 4 = 2)
❌ Never divide only the numerator when it isn't divisible — always multiply the denominator instead!
💡 Think of it physically: you have 3/4 of a pizza and split it among 6 people. Each person gets 3/(4×6) = 1/8 of the original whole pizza.

🔵 Tier A — Basic Remainder Concept
Concept

What Does "of the Remaining" Mean?

📌 Step 1: Remainder = 1 − first fraction
Step 2: Second part = second fraction × remainder  ("of" = multiply!)
Step 3: What's left = remainder − second part
Step 4: Check: all parts must add to 1 ✅
❌ Wrong: subtracting both fractions directly from 1. The second fraction is NOT of the whole — it is of the remainder only!

🟡 Tier B — Remainder + Fixed Value
Concept

When There's a Dollar Amount AND a Fraction

Some problems mix a fixed amount (like $351 or 55 stamps) with a fraction of the remainder. You cannot simply use the fraction formula — you need to treat the fixed amount separately.

🔑 Method:
Step 1: Express what is "left" as a fraction of the total.
Step 2: Set it equal to what you know (the fixed amount or fraction of original).
Step 3: Solve for the total.
💡 These are harder than Tier A because you must form an equation involving both the fixed value and the fraction.

🟣 Tier C — Equal Fractions (Two Different Wholes)
Concept

When a Fraction of A = a Fraction of B

Example: "1/3 of plastic bottles = 4/5 of aluminum cans." The two totals are different. We find the ratio using cross-multiplication.

🔑 Method:
Step 1: Write the equal fractions as an equation (e.g. P/3 = 4A/5)
Step 2: Cross-multiply to find the ratio (5P = 12A → P:A = 12:5)
Step 3: Use the difference or total to find each value
⚠️ This is different from the remainder concept! Here there are two separate totals, not one whole split into parts.

🔴 Tier D — Advanced Multi-Step Problems
Advanced

What Makes Tier D Different?

These problems combine fractions with substitution, internal transfer, or unit cost reasoning. No single method — you need to combine skills.

📋 Three sub-types in Tier D:

💰 Fraction + Substitution: "bracelet costs 3× a clip" — convert items into one unit, then use fractions.

🔄 Internal Transfer: someone gives a fraction of their amount to another, and they end up equal — use bar model with units.

📚 Unit Cost + Fraction: find cost per item from the fraction spent, then solve for the total.
